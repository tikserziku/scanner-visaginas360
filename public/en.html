🧒 Why an expensive scanner beats a cheap one — in plain words

Imagine a 3D scanner is a camera that, instead of a normal picture, makes a "cast" of an object so you can print it later. The cheap and the expensive one do the same thing, but differently:

✏️ 1. Accuracy is like the thickness of a pencil

A cheap scanner draws with a thick pencil — small details get smudged, as if you traced a coin with a fat marker. An expensive one draws with a thin one — you can see every little letter and scratch. That is why only an expensive one will capture a ring or a tiny gear well.

🏃 2. Speed — like a fast and a slow camera

An expensive one "photographs" the object tens of times faster and does not lose track even if you move your hand. A cheap one has to be moved slowly and carefully, otherwise it "loses" the object and you have to start over.

✨ 3. Shiny and black things — the hardest part

Shiny metal and black objects are to a scanner what a mirror and darkness are to the eyes: they are hard to "see". A cheap one often cannot see such surfaces at all (you have to paint them with a special spray). An expensive one with a blue laser sees them on its own.

📏 4. Both tiny and large

A cheap one captures only one kind well — either a small figurine or a large part. An expensive one can do both: from a tiny screw to an entire machine.

🔌 5. No cables and no computer

The most expensive ones work on their own, like a phone — no cables and no laptop. With a cheap one you have to drag a computer around on a cable.

📜 6. A "quality certificate" (for work)

Very expensive scanners give you a paper certificate that their measurements can be officially trusted — this is needed in factories, in aircraft, at power stations. For home use and 3D printing such a paper is not needed, so there is no point in overpaying.

🎯 The takeaway in one sentence

An expensive scanner = more accurate, faster, sees shiny and dark surfaces, works cable-free and with a quality guarantee. But if you just need to scan a toy or a part for 3D printing — an affordable one at €300–450 is more than enough. Paying thousands is only worth it when every hundredth of a millimeter and certified accuracy matter.

🎨 "Does the scanner attach color right away?" — yes, both do. But how much does it matter?

How it works: both have a built-in color camera, and the color (texture) is "applied" onto the 3D shape automatically in a single pass — no need to buy a separate camera. The output is a model not only of the right shape, but also with the figurine's coloring.

But color is NOT always needed:

  • 🟢 Color MATTERS if you want: a digital copy of a painted figurine "as is" (for archiving, rendering, showing on screen, in Blender), or printing on a color 3D printer. Then a nice texture is half the result.
  • ⚪ Color is NOT needed if you print with ordinary single-color material (PLA/resin) and paint the figurine with a brush yourself. The printer only needs the shape (geometry); it does not print the texture anyway — color is just "for looks" on screen.

On color quality the Seal is stronger: a 24-bit camera + 0.01 mm geometry = a dense, clean, accurate texture on a painted miniature. INSPIRE 2 also has color and it is fine, but it is a secondary feature — the manufacturer emphasizes the laser and geometry, not color.

🏁 What to choose

  • 🎨 You need GOOD color of a painted figurine → 3DMakerpro Seal (€473). A 24-bit camera + 0.01 mm accuracy = the best full-color result on small items. Downside: no turntable/markers in the box; dark/gloss interferes with color (spray).
  • 📐 Lightweight and cheaper, shape matters → Revopoint INSPIRE 2 (€508). The laser captures dark/shiny surfaces without spray, full kit (turntable + 200 markers), 190 g. But accuracy is below the Seal, and the laser is weaker than the POP 4's.
  • 🆕 You need an all-rounder for years to come → Revopoint POP 4 (~€750–850, wait for delivery). 30 blue laser lines beat dark/shiny surfaces best of all, high-speed modes, objects from 10 mm to 2 m. A clear upgrade over the INSPIRE 2. Downsides: pricier, not fully standalone, still pre-order in the EU.

In short: Seal — the "figurine photographer" (shape + color, excellent); INSPIRE 2 — the "lightweight workhorse for shape"; POP 4 — the "Swiss knife": pricier and a wait, but one device for any task (detail + shine + size + speed).

💬 My verdict on the POP 4

For figurines alone, the POP 4 is overpaying. If your only task is scanning figurines and small parts for printing — the Seal (for color/detail) or INSPIRE 2 (for lightness and price) will do the same, cheaper and right now, without waiting for EU stock.

People get the POP 4 for versatility: if besides figurines you will also scan shiny metal, dark parts, large objects up to 2 m and need speed — it is the best choice of the three, justifying the price and the wait. For metal reverse-engineering tasks the POP 4 is more interesting than the Seal precisely because of the blue laser. Tip: wait for it to appear in EU retail (3djake/igo3d) with a local warranty, do not buy directly from Kickstarter.

⚠️ Important caveats (from reviews)

  1. Accuracy ≠ precision. The claimed 0.01–0.02 mm is under ideal conditions. In practice the point cloud of prosumer scanners is "noisy": a flat surface looks rough in the mesh. For CAD reverse engineering this is critical — test on a real part before buying.
  2. Dark metal and matte surfaces. The blue laser (Raptor / MetroX / FreeScan) handles these better than structured light. For stainless/painted metal — use the laser modes.
  3. Markers. Large objects need reflective markers (included with the Raptor Pro). Without them, tracking drifts on uniform surfaces.
  4. Object size. Precision drops as the object grows (volumetric accuracy). A bolt and a reactor are different modes.
  5. Software. Creality Scan / Revopoint Studio / Shining3D EXStar. Check the export to STEP/STL for further CAD work.
